Correlation Between Screen Time and Academic Performance

An analysis of how daily phone usage affects GPA among high school students, using survey data from 500 participants across 3 schools.

The Problem

With smartphones becoming central to teen life, many educators and parents worry that excessive screen time is hurting academic outcomes. But is there real data to support this? This project investigates whether there is a measurable correlation between daily phone screen time and GPA among high school students.

Key Finding

Figure 1: Scatter plot of daily screen time (hours) vs. GPA for 500 students. Each point represents one student.

Key Takeaways

Finding 1

Students with 6+ hours of daily screen time had GPAs averaging 0.4 points lower than those under 2 hours.

Finding 2

The negative correlation was strongest among 10th graders (r = −0.71) and weakest among seniors (r = −0.45).

Finding 3

Students who reported using phones mostly for educational apps showed no significant GPA drop.

Conclusion

The data supports a moderate negative correlation between recreational screen time and academic performance. However, correlation does not equal causation — other factors like study habits, sleep, and socioeconomic background likely play a role. Future research should use longitudinal data to establish stronger causal claims.
